# Adaptive Systems Ontology
## Validation and Falsification Plan v0.1

## Purpose

ASO should be evaluated by trying to break it. The framework strengthens only if it survives derivation testing, minimality testing, counterexample testing, domain-specific validation, and expert critique.

The goal is not to protect the framework. The goal is to let reality correct it.

## Validation types

### 1. Logical validation

Question:

> Is the architecture internally coherent?

Tests:

- Can each derived concept be generated from the primitives?
- Can any primitive be removed without loss?
- Do concepts overlap unnecessarily?
- Can two independent readers distinguish one concept from another?

### 2. Cross-domain validation

Question:

> Can the same functional structure describe different domains without forcing the fit?

Domains to test:

- physics
- chemistry
- biology
- genetics
- immune systems
- cognition
- psychology
- AI
- business
- governance
- law
- economics
- culture
- ecosystems
- civilization

### 3. Empirical validation

Question:

> Does ASO improve prediction, classification, or explanation in at least one domain beyond existing approaches?

Each domain requires its own accepted methods. Human research requires ethical safeguards and domain expertise. AI validation uses model benchmarks and training metrics. Organizational validation uses operational data, governance outcomes, resilience metrics, and failure analysis. Evolutionary validation uses population and selection data.

## Falsification tests

### Test 1: Primitive insufficiency

Try to derive every required concept from the four primitives.

Failure condition:

> A necessary concept cannot be derived from existence, difference, interaction, and ordered change.

Possible result:

> Add a fifth primitive or revise definitions.

### Test 2: Primitive non-minimality

Try removing each primitive.

Failure condition:

> A primitive can be removed without breaking derivations.

Possible result:

> Reduce the primitive set.

### Test 3: Constraint pressure test

Question:

> Is constraint primitive or derived?

Current result:

> Derived. Constraint is a stable difference in structure, state, or interaction that limits possible future processes.

Failure condition:

> A form of constraint is found that cannot be explained as stable limiting difference, structure, interaction, or ordered change.

### Test 4: Information pressure test

Question:

> Is information primitive or derived?

Current result:

> Derived. Information is a difference that makes a difference to a system across ordered change.

Failure condition:

> Information exists without difference, interaction, or ordered change.

### Test 5: Response/adaptation boundary test

Question:

> Does the system merely respond, or does feedback change future response?

Failure condition:

> The rubric cannot distinguish a responsive system from an adaptive system.

### Test 6: Domain-forcing test

Question:

> Does the framework clarify a domain, or does it distort that domain to fit itself?

Failure condition:

> Domain experts find that ASO misrepresents the domain or adds no useful clarity.

## Testable predictions

### Prediction 1: Feedback failure

Systems that receive information but cannot alter future process rules will degrade under changing environments.

### Prediction 2: Representation drift

Systems whose internal representation diverges from relevant reality will produce increasingly maladaptive responses.

### Prediction 3: Delayed feedback instability

Adaptive systems with long feedback delays will overcorrect, undercorrect, or oscillate.

### Prediction 4: Process coupling risk

Highly coupled systems can adapt faster under accurate feedback but fail faster under corrupted feedback.

### Prediction 5: Recursive fragility

Failure in lower-level adaptive subsystems can propagate upward into larger systems.

Examples:

- cellular failure -> organ failure -> organism failure
- employee failure -> team failure -> organizational failure
- agency failure -> institutional failure -> government failure

## Success criteria

ASO gains support if it:

1. Derives known systems concepts cleanly from fewer primitives.
2. Clarifies relationships among existing theories.
3. Creates useful cross-domain translation.
4. Produces testable distinctions, especially response vs. adaptation.
5. Predicts system failure, resilience, or adaptation better than simpler descriptions in at least one domain.
6. Revises itself when evidence contradicts it.

## Failure criteria

ASO weakens if it:

1. Cannot derive essential concepts.
2. Requires hidden assumptions not listed as primitives.
3. Collapses into vague metaphor.
4. Replaces domain expertise with broad language.
5. Fails to improve prediction or classification.
6. Cannot distinguish response from adaptation.
7. Cannot be used consistently by independent evaluators.

## Current validation status

Status: first-pass logical derivation only.

ASO has not yet completed empirical validation, domain-expert review, or predictive comparison.

## Governing rule

> The framework must always be more willing to change than the evidence.
