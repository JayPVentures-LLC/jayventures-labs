# Adaptive Systems Ontology v0.1

## Subtitle

A Four-Primitives Framework for Deriving Structure, Process, Feedback, Persistence, Adaptation, Reflection, and Recursive Design

## Status

This document is a v0.1 research artifact. It is experimental, falsifiable, and subject to revision. It is not a production governance standard, diagnostic system, legal theory, or replacement for domain-specific science.

## Abstract

Adaptive Systems Ontology (ASO) proposes a compact ontology for deriving and comparing systems concepts across domains. The framework begins with four candidate primitives: existence, difference, interaction, and ordered change. From these, ASO attempts to derive boundary, identity, relation, structure, state, process, information, representation, feedback, persistence, response, adaptation, learning, evolution, reflection, and recursive design.

The purpose is not to claim that existing fields are wrong or redundant. Systems theory, cybernetics, information theory, process philosophy, control theory, evolutionary theory, psychology, neuroscience, and AI research each retain their domain authority. ASO is a proposed translation layer beneath those frameworks. It asks whether the same functional roots can explain why different domains repeatedly produce analogous concepts: structure, process, state, signal, feedback, learning, resilience, continuity, reform, and redesign.

## 1. Core question

The central question is:

> What is the smallest set of irreducible concepts from which adaptive-system concepts can be derived?

This question emerged from progressively reducing broader behavioral and systems claims:

- Human behavior is not merely action; it is often preservation under perceived constraint.
- Adaptation is not merely response; it requires feedback to change future response.
- Determination is not universal by itself; it is the human expression of persistence through intentional effort.
- Persistence is more foundational than adaptation.
- Interaction is more foundational than persistence because interaction makes process, information, feedback, and change possible.

The resulting candidate ontology is:

1. Existence
2. Difference
3. Interaction
4. Ordered Change

## 2. Candidate primitives

### 2.1 Existence

Existence is the possibility of being.

Without existence, there is no system, relation, structure, process, state, information, or change.

Minimal statement:

> Something is.

### 2.2 Difference

Difference is the possibility of distinction.

Without difference, there is no identity, boundary, comparison, measurement, state change, or information.

Minimal statement:

> Something can be distinguished.

### 2.3 Interaction

Interaction is the possibility of influence.

Without interaction, distinguishable entities may exist, but nothing affects anything else. There is no process, causation, feedback, adaptation, learning, or evolution.

Minimal statement:

> Something affects something else.

### 2.4 Ordered Change

Ordered Change is the possibility of sequence: before, during, after.

This does not require human timekeeping. It requires only ordered state transition. Without ordered change, there is no process, memory, feedback, learning, adaptation, or evolution.

Minimal statement:

> Something can become different in sequence.

## 3. Derived concepts

### Boundary

Boundary is derived from existence and difference.

A boundary exists when one thing can be distinguished from another.

### Identity

Identity is derived from existence, difference, and ordered change.

Identity requires distinguishability that persists or remains traceable across sequence.

### Relation

Relation is derived from difference and interaction.

A relation exists when distinguishable entities can be compared, connected, or made relevant to one another.

### Structure

Structure is derived from existence, difference, interaction, and ordered change.

Structure is stable organization among distinguishable parts or states.

### State

State is derived from structure and ordered change.

A state is the measurable condition of a structure at a point in an ordered sequence.

### Process

Process is derived from interaction and ordered change.

A process is an ordered transformation from one state to another.

`P: S_t -> S_{t+1}`

### Information

Information is derived from difference, interaction, and ordered change.

Information is a difference that can affect a system's state, process, or representation.

Short form:

> Information is a difference that makes a difference to a system.

### Representation

Representation is derived from information and structure.

A representation is stored structure that corresponds to information about something else.

Examples include memory, DNA, model weights, maps, legal precedent, market prices, organizational records, and cultural narratives.

### Feedback

Feedback is derived from process, information, and ordered change.

Feedback occurs when the result of one process becomes information for a later process.

### Persistence

Persistence is derived from structure, process, and ordered change.

Persistence occurs when sustaining processes maintain enough structure across sequence for the system to remain identifiable.

### Response

Response is derived from information and process.

Response occurs when information changes current state or action.

### Process Modification

Process Modification is derived from process and ordered change.

Process Modification occurs when the transformation rule mapping one state to a later state is changed.

### Adaptation

Adaptation is derived from feedback, persistence, and Process Modification.

Adaptation occurs when feedback changes the future response rule.

`P_{t+1} != P_t`

Short form:

> Response changes the current state. Adaptation changes the future rule.

### Learning

Learning is derived from adaptation and representation.

Learning occurs when feedback changes representation in a way that alters future response.

### Replication

Replication is derived from structure, process, and ordered change.

Replication occurs when a traceably similar structure, pattern, or rule is produced across sequence.

### Selection

Selection is derived from difference, interaction, constraint (see working definition in derivation-map.md), and ordered change.

Selection occurs when environmental conditions produce differential persistence or replication among alternatives.

### Evolution

Evolution is derived from adaptation, replication, selection, and ordered change.

Evolution occurs when adaptive differences accumulate across generations, versions, institutions, or populations.

### Recursion

Recursion is derived from representation and process.

Recursion occurs when a process operates on its own output, representation, or process structure.

### Reflection

Reflection is derived from representation and recursion.

Reflection occurs when a system represents itself or its own processes.

### Intentional Process Modification

Intentional Process Modification is derived from reflection, evaluation (see adaptive-classification-rubric.md#evaluation-checklist), and Process Modification.

Intentional Process Modification occurs when a reflective system deliberately revises a process based on represented goals, evidence, or expected consequences.

### Recursive design

Recursive design is derived from reflection, adaptation, and Intentional Process Modification.

Recursive design occurs when a system modifies the process by which it modifies itself.

Examples include science, education, governance reform, engineering, and AI system design.

## 4. Three-transform adaptive model

Within the broader ontology, adaptive systems can be compressed into three transformations.

### Information transformation

Reality becomes usable information.

Question:

> What is known?

### State transformation

Information produces action or state change.

Question:

> What changes now?

### System transformation

Feedback changes the system's future response pattern.

Question:

> What changes next time?

## 5. Boundary between response and adaptation

A system can respond without adapting.

A basic thermostat responds to temperature, but does not meaningfully update its future rule. A learning thermostat can adapt if feedback changes future settings. A large language model during ordinary inference responds to context, but does not update its base parameters. An AI training pipeline adapts because feedback modifies future behavior through parameter updates.

Strong boundary:

> Context change is not enough. Rule change is required.

## 6. Persistence and determination

Determination is not treated as a universal primitive. It is a human-level implementation of persistence.

Universal function:

> Persistence

Human implementation:

> Determination

Biological implementation:

> Homeostasis and survival regulation

Organizational implementation:

> Resilience and operational continuity

Civilizational implementation:

> Institutional continuity and reform capacity

AI implementation:

> Robustness, fault tolerance, and sustained optimization within designed conditions

Determination belongs inside evaluation for reflective agents because it describes how much effort an agent is willing to invest toward preserving or achieving a valued state.

## 7. Humans, animals, technology, and nature

The process structure can be shared while the implementation differs.

### Humans

Humans implement adaptive architecture through biology, memory, language, emotion, abstraction, reflection, values, culture, and intentional redesign.

Humans are high-recursion implementations because they can represent reality, represent themselves, represent their own representations, externalize models into language and institutions, and redesign the systems that shape adaptation.

### Animals

Animals adapt through sensory processing, learning, instinct, memory, reinforcement, group behavior, and evolution. Some animals show limited self-recognition or metacognitive capacity, but the degree varies by species.

### Technology

Technology executes or learns within designed constraints. It can process information, produce outputs, and adapt when training, updating, or feedback mechanisms are built into the system. Most current technology does not possess autonomous reflective redesign except through human-designed processes or limited automated optimization.

### Nature

Nature includes physical, chemical, ecological, and evolutionary processes. Natural systems can persist, respond, adapt, and evolve without central awareness. Ecosystems adapt through distributed interactions, feedback loops, selection pressures, and resource cycles.

## 8. Adaptive capability hierarchy

The framework distinguishes levels of capability:

0. Existence: something is.
1. Response: the system reacts.
2. Adaptation: feedback changes future response.
3. Representation: the system maintains internal or stored models.
4. Reflection: the system models itself.
5. Meta-adaptation: the system redesigns how it adapts.
6. Recursive civilization: systems redesign systems that redesign systems.

Each level contains the prior levels but adds new capability.

## 9. Complexity hypothesis

Complexity is not merely the number of parts.

Working hypothesis:

> Complexity increases with the number, diversity, coupling, and feedback sensitivity of interacting processes.

A pile of rocks may contain many parts but few interacting processes. A cell may contain fewer visible parts but far denser process interaction and feedback. Under this framework, the cell is more complex in adaptive-process terms.

This is a hypothesis requiring measurement.

## 10. Comparison to existing frameworks

### General systems theory

General systems theory studies systems as interconnected wholes. ASO does not replace it. ASO attempts to operate beneath the level of system by deriving system concepts from more primitive foundations.

### Cybernetics

Cybernetics studies feedback, regulation, communication, circular causality, and control. ASO treats feedback as a derived concept from process, information, and ordered change.

### Information theory

Information theory formalizes information mathematically. ASO does not replace it. ASO provides a conceptual derivation of information as a difference that affects a system.

### Process philosophy

Process philosophy emphasizes becoming, relation, and change. ASO is adjacent but more operational: it asks what primitives are required to derive systems, feedback, adaptation, reflection, and recursive design.

### Control theory

Control theory assumes systems, targets, signals, outputs, feedback, and correction. ASO attempts to derive the conditions that make those assumptions possible.

### Evolutionary theory

Evolutionary theory remains the domain-specific scientific authority for biological adaptation across populations. ASO treats evolution as one derived implementation of difference, interaction, ordered change, persistence, selection, and accumulated adaptation.

## 11. Current strongest claim

The strongest defensible claim is:

> Adaptive Systems Ontology proposes a minimal ontology from which major systems concepts can be derived and compared across domains.

It should not claim to explain everything, replace existing sciences, or collapse all domains into one mechanism.

## 12. Falsification standard

ASO weakens if:

- a derived concept cannot be derived from the proposed primitives;
- a primitive can be removed without loss;
- a domain requires a fifth irreducible primitive;
- the framework merely renames existing concepts without improving clarity, comparison, or prediction;
- the adaptive classification fails to distinguish response from adaptation;
- domain experts cannot use it without forcing the fit.

ASO strengthens if:

- the primitive set remains minimal;
- derived concepts remain coherent;
- cross-domain mappings clarify rather than obscure;
- predictions about adaptation, failure, drift, and resilience outperform simpler descriptions in at least one domain;
- the framework remains willing to revise itself when evidence contradicts it.

## 13. Ethical boundary for human use

ASO may be used to study process architecture, but it must not be used as a standalone tool to judge people, diagnose people, rank moral worth, assign blame, deny opportunity, or excuse harm. Human implementation requires ethical review, domain expertise, consent standards where applicable, and people-protection constraints.

## 14. Working theorem candidates

### Theorem candidate 1

A system becomes adaptive when persistent structure uses feedback from prior interactions to modify future processes across ordered change.

Short form:

> Adaptation is feedback-modified process persistence.

### Theorem candidate 2

A difference becomes information when it interacts with a system in a way that can affect future state or process.

### Theorem candidate 3

Response changes the environment or current state; adaptation changes the responder's future rule.

### Theorem candidate 4

Determination is persistence expressed through reflective evaluation and intentional effort.

## 15. Current status

ASO v0.1 is a candidate ontology. The next work is not expansion. The next work is reduction, derivation testing, counterexample testing, and domain-specific validation.
