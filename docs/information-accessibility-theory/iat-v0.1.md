# Information Accessibility Theory v0.1

## Subtitle

A Companion Framework to Adaptive Systems Ontology for Studying Information Availability, Intuition, Insight, Memory Access, Dreaming, Deja Vu, and Conscious Explanation

## Status

This document is a v0.1 research artifact. It is experimental, falsifiable, and subject to revision. It is not a clinical framework, spiritual doctrine, diagnostic instrument, or proof of any specific unexplained mechanism.

## Abstract

Information Accessibility Theory (IAT) proposes that information, representation, accessibility, consciousness, expression, and application should be treated as distinct states or transitions. The central claim is not that intuition has a single cause. The central claim is that several phenomena often treated separately may involve changes in information accessibility: memory retrieval, tip-of-the-tongue states, sudden insight, dreaming, deja vu, creative breakthroughs, intuition, and reports of knowing before explanation.

IAT is a companion framework to Adaptive Systems Ontology (ASO). ASO asks what functions make adaptive systems possible. IAT asks how information becomes available within those systems.

## 1. Core question

The core research question is:

> What determines when information becomes available to an adaptive system?

This question is intentionally mechanism-neutral. It does not assume that information is internally generated, externally acquired, unconsciously processed, spiritually sourced, anomalous, or produced by a mechanism not yet characterized. It first asks what changed when information became available.

## 2. Foundational distinction

IAT begins with the distinction:

> Information may exist without being accessible.

A memory can exist but not be recalled. A skill can be performed but not verbally explained. A solution can emerge after sleep even though the problem was encountered earlier. A person can report certainty before explanation.

This requires a distinction between information state and accessibility state.

## 3. Key terms

### Information

A difference that can affect a system's state, process, or representation.

### Representation

Stored structure corresponding to information about something else.

### Accessibility

The degree to which represented or potential information is available for system use.

### Conscious availability

A state in which information is available to subjective awareness or deliberate reasoning.

### Explanation

A linguistic, symbolic, or structured account of why information is believed, known, or relevant.

### Intuition

A reported state in which information, certainty, or direction becomes available before conscious explanation.

### Deja vu

A reported state or transition involving familiarity, recognition, or prior-experience sensation without ordinary conscious identification of the source.

## 4. Accessibility states

IAT proposes a draft sequence of information states:

```text
Potential Information
  -> Available Information
  -> Represented Information
  -> Integrated Information
  -> Latent Information
  -> Accessible Information
  -> Consciously Available Information
  -> Expressed Information
  -> Applied Information
  -> Feedback
  -> Adaptation
```

The sequence is not strictly linear. Information may cycle, skip states, decay, become inaccessible, or become available under specific conditions.

## 5. Accessibility gates

Accessibility may be affected by gates. These are not final categories, only first-pass candidates.

### Biological gates

- sleep
- circadian rhythm
- fatigue
- hormonal state
- illness
- nutrition
- stress physiology

### Cognitive gates

- attention
- priming
- working memory load
- context
- existing representations
- pattern similarity
- schema activation

### Emotional gates

- fear
- safety
- attachment
- grief
- urgency
- salience
- motivation

### Environmental gates

- place
- sound
- smell
- light
- people
- objects
- timing

### Unknown gates

Mechanisms not yet characterized.

Unknown is not an explanation. It is a placeholder for evidence not yet explained by tested models.

## 6. Phenomena addressed by IAT

IAT organizes several phenomena as possible accessibility transitions.

### Memory cueing

A memory becomes accessible only after a cue changes retrieval conditions.

### Tip-of-the-tongue states

Information is partially accessible, but expression is blocked or incomplete.

### Shower insights

A solution becomes accessible after attention, stress, or cognitive load shifts.

### Dreams

Information may be reorganized, recombined, or made temporarily accessible under altered conscious conditions.

### Insight after sleep

Sleep may alter representation, integration, or accessibility.

### Intuition

A conclusion, direction, or certainty becomes available before conscious explanation.

### Deja vu

A familiarity or prior-experience signal becomes available without ordinary source identification.

## 7. Intuition as information availability

The core intuition question is:

> Where does the information come from, and why does it become available at that moment?

IAT separates three questions:

1. Where did the information originate?
2. How did it enter or arise within the system?
3. Why did it become consciously available when it did?

These must not be collapsed.

For example, a reported intuition may involve unknown origin, unknown entry pathway, and immediate conscious availability. That does not prove any one explanation. It defines the research problem.

## 8. Deja vu as a research object

IAT does not reduce deja vu to one explanation. It treats deja vu as a candidate accessibility event.

Competing models include:

- memory-source mismatch
- prediction-processing anomaly
- representation similarity exceeding threshold
- temporal-ordering disruption
- latent information becoming briefly accessible
- past-life or prior-existence memory hypothesis
- unknown mechanism

The past-life hypothesis is not accepted as established. It remains a possible explanatory model only if it generates distinguishable predictions and survives comparison against stronger ordinary explanations.

## 9. Unknown-origin information

Some experiences are reported as information appearing without identifiable cause.

IAT does not dismiss those reports. It also does not treat them as proof of a preferred explanation.

Proper handling requires:

- prospective documentation
- timestamped records
- specific predictions
- alternative explanations
- outcome tracking
- failure tracking
- independent review where possible

## 10. Relationship to consciousness

IAT treats consciousness as an accessibility condition, not as identical to information.

Information may be:

- outside awareness
- partially accessible
- consciously available
- verbally expressible
- action-guiding

This distinction helps explain why someone may know before they can explain, act before they can verbalize, or feel certainty before conscious reasoning is available.

## 11. Relationship to ASO

ASO defines adaptive functions.

IAT investigates one critical interface inside adaptive systems:

> How information becomes available for representation, evaluation, action, feedback, and adaptation.

Without accessible information, there is no deliberate reasoning, no conscious decision-making, and limited adaptive correction.

## 12. Research standards

IAT strengthens if it can:

- distinguish information state from accessibility state;
- classify different accessibility transitions;
- measure conditions that increase or decrease accessibility;
- compare ordinary and unknown-mechanism explanations;
- prospectively test intuition reports;
- explain why some information becomes conscious before explanation.

IAT weakens if it:

- collapses all intuition into one cause;
- treats unknown as proof;
- relies only on hindsight cases;
- ignores failed intuitions;
- cannot distinguish memory, insight, prediction, and intuition;
- fails to produce measurable differences between accessibility states.

## 13. Current strongest claim

The strongest defensible claim is:

> Information accessibility is a distinct adaptive-system variable that may explain why information can exist, be represented, and still remain unavailable until biological, cognitive, emotional, environmental, or unknown conditions change.

## 14. Current status

IAT v0.1 is a candidate companion framework. It requires observation protocols, case logging, measurement design, counterexample testing, and comparison against existing cognitive science and neuroscience models.
